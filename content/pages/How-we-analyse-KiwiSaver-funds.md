# How we analyse KiwiSaver funds

This page sets out our methodology. It explains what we assess, how we compare funds, and the criteria a fund must meet to reach our recommended list.

There are hundreds of KiwiSaver funds available in New Zealand. Below is exactly how we compare them, and how we decide which ones to recommend.

## The principles behind our analysis

#### 1. Like for like comparisons

A Growth fund should not be compared against a Conservative fund. It is not a fair comparison and it produces misleading conclusions. KiwiSaver funds generally sit in one of five risk groups, determined mainly by asset allocation, what the fund invests in, such as cash, bonds, shares, and property:

* Defensive, typically mostly cash and bonds
* Conservative, mostly income assets with a small allocation to growth assets
* Balanced, a roughly even blend of growth and income assets
* Growth, mostly growth assets with some income assets
* Aggressive, almost entirely growth assets, such as shares

We compare funds only against other funds in the same risk group, so every fund is judged against relevant peers.

#### 2. Long-term evidence beats short-term noise

Past returns do not guarantee future returns, but they do show how a fund has performed across different market conditions. We focus on longer timeframes, typically five to ten years where available, treat anything less with caution, and exclude funds altogether if they don’t have at least three years’ worth of data. The aim is to avoid being swayed by a fund’s latest hot streak.

#### 3. We look beyond a single number

Returns alone are not enough. We also assess fees, consistency and downside behaviour (volatility), and how the fund is run. The aim is to identify funds that are consistently better than its peers, not last year’s winner.

## The four core checks we apply to every KiwiSaver fund

We apply a consistent set of checks across four areas. The goal is simple: recommend funds that have a strong performance track record, fair fees, and are well run.

#### Performance over meaningful timeframes

We look at performance over longer periods, typically five to ten years where available, rather than the most recent quarter or year. We look at returns after fees, and we also assess those returns on a risk-adjusted basis, meaning we consider the level of volatility a fund took on to achieve its returns. A fund that delivered similar returns with a smoother ride is doing more with less risk.

#### Fees and value

Fees matter, and they compound over decades, but the cheapest fund is not always the best. We look at the total annual fund charge and assess whether it is reasonable for what the fund is trying to deliver. A passive, index-tracking fund should be priced very differently from an actively managed fund, and our view is both have a role to play. The question we are really asking is: has this fund earned its fee, after fees, versus its peers?

#### Consistency and downside risk

We look at how bumpy the ride has been and how the fund has held up when markets have been rough, including during past downturns. This includes assessing the size of past drawdowns and how the fund behaves relative to its peer group in weak markets. The aim is to avoid surprises. A fund that looks strong in a bull market but falls harder than its peers when markets turn is not necessarily one worth holding long term.

#### Quality and red flag checks

Numbers alone are not enough. We also look at practical quality signals, such as:

* Does the fund invest how they say they will
* Is the fund genuinely diversified, or overly concentrated in a narrow sector, region, or theme
* Is the fund of a reasonable scale, with enough assets under management to operate efficiently
* Are the managers and strategy stable, or has there been significant turnover
* Are there any obvious red flags in how the fund is run, disclosed, or communicated

## How we grade funds

Every fund we assess is given an overall grade based on how it performs across the four core checks. Grades run from A+ down to F. In practice, only around the top 10% of funds we analyse reach a grade of B or above and make our recommended list.

Grade	Public label	Status	What it means

A+	Top pick	Recommended	Stands out against peers across all four checks.

A	Top performer	Recommended	Performs well across all four checks.

B	Quality option	Recommended	Meets our bar across all four checks.

C	Not recommended	Not recommended	Falls short on our analysis.

D	Not recommended	Not recommended	Falls short on our analysis.

E	Not recommended	Not recommended	Falls short on our analysis.

F	Not recommended	Not recommended	Falls short on our analysis.

Every fund we assess is displayed publicly with its grade, whether or not it makes our recommended list. Transparency cuts both ways — it would not be useful to only show the funds we like.

We keep the recommended list intentionally tight. The aim is a shortlist we are comfortable advising clients into, while still allowing for different preferences.

A fund being rated C or below does not automatically mean it is a bad fund. It means that, on our criteria, we are not prepared to recommend it today. The most common reasons a fund lands outside A+, A, or B are:

* It did not score strongly enough against its peers in the same risk group
* There was not enough reliable data to assess it, for example a limited track record
* It is too narrowly focused, which can increase risk through lack of diversification
* There are quality concerns around how the fund is run, disclosed, or communicated
* It is a fund-of-fund that simply invests into another fund, without offering meaningful benefits over going directly to the underlying fund manager

## How often we review

Whilst KiwiSaver is a long-term investment for most people, funds change. Markets change. Fees change. Managers and strategies can change. We refresh our analysis at a minimum on an annual basis.

## Where our data comes from

The first data source we rely on is Morningstar, which we have a data relationship with. If we find holes in the data from Morningstar, we then look at the Disclose Register, documents such as Quarterly Fund Updates and Product Disclosure Statements, and finally provider websites if we need additional information. At times we also engage directly with fund providers if we can’t find answers to our questions from the other data sources.

## Commissions and independence

BetterSaver may receive a commission from some KiwiSaver providers if you join or switch into their fund through our platform. This does not increase your fund fees.

Commissions do not influence our analysis or our recommendations. In practice, there are more funds from providers we could be paid for that do not make our recommended list than there are funds on our recommended list. That is because the list is driven by our criteria, not commercial arrangements.

Our analysis can include funds that pay us and funds that do not, as long as they meet our standards.

Where we do recommend a provider’s funds, we aim to have a commercial agreement in place so we can deliver the best experience for clients, including smoother switching, better support, and the ability to keep investing in our advice and tools.

If a provider does not support switching through our platform, you may need to complete your signup or switch directly on the provider’s website. We will make that clear before you proceed.

## Frequently asked questions

What do BetterSaver’s fund grades mean?

We grade every fund from A+ down to F based on how it performs across our four core checks. Funds rated A+ (Top pick), A (Top performer), and B (Quality option) are on our recommended list — typically around the top 10% of funds we analyse. Funds rated C, D, E, or F are labelled Not recommended, but we still display their grade publicly so the full picture is visible.

What KiwiSaver fees should I look for when comparing funds?

The main fee to watch is the total annual fund charge (sometimes shown as the total expense ratio). We look at whether fees are reasonable relative to performance — a fund with higher fees needs to demonstrably justify the cost over time.

How does BetterSaver compare KiwiSaver funds?

We assess every fund across four core checks — long-term performance, fees, consistency, and quality. We then match funds to the right risk level for each person, so comparisons are always like-for-like.

How far back does BetterSaver look at KiwiSaver fund performance?

We focus on performance over meaningful timeframes — typically five to ten years where available. We exclude funds with less than three years of data, and treat anything under five years with caution.

How is a KiwiSaver fund comparison different from picking the best-performing fund?

The best-performing fund over the last year is not necessarily the right fund for you. A meaningful comparison accounts for your risk level, your investment horizon, and fee impact over time — not just a single return figure.

Where does BetterSaver source its KiwiSaver fund data?

Our primary data source is Morningstar, which we have a data relationship with. We supplement this with the Disclose Register, Quarterly Fund Updates, Product Disclosure Statements, provider websites, and direct engagement with fund providers where needed.

How often does BetterSaver update its fund recommendations?

We refresh our analysis at a minimum on an annual basis. If a fund’s quality, fees, or strategy change materially between reviews, it can be added or removed at any time.
